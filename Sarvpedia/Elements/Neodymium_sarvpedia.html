Neodymium

Introduction

Neodymium is a chemical element with the symbol Nd and atomic number 60. It is a soft, silvery-white metal that is part of the lanthanide series of the periodic table. Neodymium is known for its strong magnetic properties and is used in the production of permanent magnets.

The name "neodymium" is derived from the Greek words "neos" and "didymos," meaning "new twin," as it was discovered along with another element, praseodymium. Neodymium was discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach.

Isotopes

Neodymium has several isotopes, with 142Nd being the most abundant, accounting for about 27% of natural neodymium. The average atomic mass of neodymium is approximately 144.242 u. Neodymium-143 (143Nd) is a radioactive isotope used in geological dating.

Properties

Neodymium is a soft, silvery-white metal with a high melting point. It is relatively stable in air and does not oxidize easily. Neodymium exhibits both metallic and non-metallic properties, making it unique among the lanthanides.

Physical Properties

Neodymium has a density of 7.01 g/cm³, a melting point of 1024°C (1875°F), and a boiling point of 3074°C (5565°F). Its crystalline structure is hexagonal close-packed.

Chemical Properties

Neodymium reacts with water and acids, forming neodymium hydroxide and neodymium salts. It exhibits oxidation states of +2, +3, and +4, with the +3 state being the most stable.

History

Neodymium was discovered in 1885 by Carl Auer von Welsbach, who isolated it from a sample of didymium. The element was later confirmed by other chemists, including Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran. Neodymium has since been used in various applications, particularly in the production of permanent magnets.
