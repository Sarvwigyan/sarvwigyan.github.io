Antimony

Introduction

Antimony is a chemical element with the symbol Sb (from Latin "stibium") and atomic number 51. It is a metalloid in group 15 of the periodic table, displaying properties between metals and nonmetals. Antimony occurs naturally in the Earth’s crust at about 0.2 ppm, primarily in the mineral stibnite.

Isotopes

Antimony has two naturally occurring stable isotopes: 121Sb, making up 57.21% of its abundance, and 123Sb, comprising 42.79%. It also has several radioactive isotopes, such as 125Sb, with a half-life of 2.76 years.

Properties

Antimony is a brittle, silvery-gray metalloid with a crystalline structure. It is a poor conductor of heat and electricity. Antimony melts at 630.63°C (1167.13°F) and boils at 1587°C (2889°F).

History

Antimony’s use dates back millennia, evolving from ancient alchemy to modern metallurgy.

Early Observations

Antimony compounds were used in ancient Egypt as kohl for cosmetics and in Mesopotamia for medicinal purposes, though the element itself was not isolated.

The Discovery

Antimony was recognized as an element in the 17th century, with its isolation credited to European alchemists like Vannoccio Biringuccio (1540). Its name derives from "stibium," linked to the mineral stibnite.

Periodic Table Impact

Antimony’s placement in group 15 bridges metalloids and nonmetals, akin to arsenic and bismuth.

Applications

Antimony’s unique properties support a range of practical uses:

1. Alloys

Hardens lead in batteries, bullets, and type metal for printing.

2. Flame Retardants

Antimony trioxide enhances fire resistance in plastics and textiles.

3. Semiconductors

Used in infrared detectors and diodes.

4. Pigments

Provides yellow and white hues in paints and ceramics.

5. Medicine

Historically used in treatments; modern use is limited but includes some antiparasitic drugs.

6. Glass

Acts as a fining agent to remove bubbles in glass production.

Antimony in the Universe

Antimony is rare in the cosmos, formed through stellar nucleosynthesis and detected in trace amounts in meteorites and interstellar dust.

Modern Production

Antimony is primarily extracted from stibnite ore via roasting and reduction processes. China is the leading producer, followed by Russia and Bolivia.

Safety

Antimony and its compounds are toxic, causing irritation to skin, eyes, and lungs. Chronic exposure may lead to more severe health effects, necessitating careful handling.

Conclusion

Antimony, a metalloid with ancient roots and modern utility, exemplifies the blend of historical significance and industrial versatility.
