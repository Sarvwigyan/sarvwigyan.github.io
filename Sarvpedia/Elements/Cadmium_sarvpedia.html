Cadmium

Introduction

Cadmium is a chemical element with the symbol Cd and atomic number 48. It is a soft, silvery-white metal in the transition metal group of the periodic table. Cadmium occurs naturally in the Earth's crust and is often found as a byproduct of zinc ore processing.

Isotopes

Cadmium has eight naturally occurring isotopes, with 114Cd (28.73%) and 112Cd (24.13%) being the most abundant. It also has several radioactive isotopes, such as 109Cd, with a half-life of 462.6 days.

Properties

Cadmium is a soft, malleable, and ductile metal with a bluish-white hue. It resists corrosion but oxidizes in moist air, forming a protective oxide layer. Its melting point is 321°C (610°F), and its boiling point is 767°C (1413°F).

History

Cadmium’s discovery added a new dimension to the understanding of metals in the periodic table.

Early Observations

Cadmium was first noticed as an impurity in zinc compounds in the early 19th century.

The Discovery

Cadmium was discovered in 1817 by German chemist Friedrich Stromeyer and independently by Karl Samuel Leberecht Hermann. It was identified as a distinct element in zinc carbonate samples and named after the Latin word "cadmia," meaning zinc ore.

Periodic Table Impact

Cadmium’s placement in the periodic table helped refine the classification of transition metals.

Applications

Cadmium’s unique properties enable a variety of industrial uses:

1. Batteries

Used in nickel-cadmium (NiCd) rechargeable batteries due to its high energy density.

2. Pigments

Employed in vibrant yellow, orange, and red pigments for paints and plastics.

3. Electroplating

Applied as a corrosion-resistant coating on steel and other metals.

4. Nuclear Reactors

Used in control rods due to its ability to absorb neutrons effectively.

Cadmium in the Universe

Cadmium is formed in the universe through the slow neutron capture process (s-process) in stars and is dispersed into space via supernovae, eventually becoming part of planetary crusts like Earth’s.

Modern Production

Cadmium is primarily obtained as a byproduct of zinc refining. It is extracted during the smelting and purification of zinc ores, such as sphalerite, and separated through distillation or electrolysis.

Safety

Cadmium is highly toxic and poses significant health risks, including kidney damage and cancer, if inhaled or ingested. Its use is heavily regulated due to environmental and health concerns.

Conclusion

Cadmium, a naturally occurring yet toxic metal, showcases a balance between industrial utility and environmental challenges.
