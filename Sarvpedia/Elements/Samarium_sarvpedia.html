Samarium

Introduction

Samarium is a chemical element with the symbol Sm and atomic number 62. It is a silvery-white metal that is part of the lanthanide series of the periodic table. Samarium is known for its use in various applications, including magnets, nuclear reactors, and as a neutron absorber.

The element was discovered in 1853 by the French chemist Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran, who isolated it from samarskite, a mineral containing rare earth elements. The name "samarium" is derived from the mineral samarskite.

Isotopes

Samarium has several isotopes, with 144Sm being the most stable and abundant, accounting for about 3% of natural samarium. The average atomic mass of samarium is approximately 150.36 u. Samarium-147 (147Sm) is a radioactive isotope with a half-life of about 2.62 million years and is used in dating geological formations.

Properties

Samarium is a hard, silvery metal that is relatively stable in air. It can oxidize when exposed to moisture and is known for its magnetic properties.

Physical Properties

Samarium has a density of 7.52 g/cm³, a melting point of 1072°C (1962°F), and a boiling point of 1794°C (3261°F). Its crystalline structure is hexagonal close-packed.

Chemical Properties

Samarium reacts with oxygen to form samarium oxide (Sm₂O₃) and can also react with acids to form samarium salts. It exhibits oxidation states of +2 and +3, with the +3 state being the most stable.

Samarium is used in the production of samarium-cobalt magnets, which are known for their high strength and resistance to demagnetization.

2. Nuclear Reactors

Samarium is used as a neutron absorber in nuclear reactors, helping to control the fission process.

3. Research

Samarium is used in various scientific research applications, particularly in studies related to rare earth elements and their properties.

4. Dating Geological Formations

Samarium-147 is used in radiometric dating to determine the age of geological formations and meteorites.
