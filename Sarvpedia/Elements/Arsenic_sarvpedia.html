Arsenic

Introduction

Arsenic is a chemical element with the symbol As and atomic number 33. It is a metalloid in group 15 of the periodic table, exhibiting properties between metals and nonmetals. Arsenic is relatively common in the Earth’s crust, occurring at about 1.5 ppm, primarily in minerals like arsenopyrite.

Isotopes

Arsenic has one naturally occurring stable isotope, 75As, which constitutes 100% of its natural abundance. Radioactive isotopes, such as 76As with a half-life of 1.094 days, exist but decay rapidly.

Properties

Arsenic is a brittle, grayish metalloid in its most stable form, though it can also appear as a yellow or black allotrope. It sublimes at 615°C (1139°F) rather than melting under standard pressure and is a poor conductor of electricity.

History

Arsenic’s notoriety spans from ancient poison to modern utility, rooted in its chemical versatility.

Early Observations

Arsenic compounds were known in antiquity, used by the Egyptians and Greeks as pigments and medicines, though the element itself was not isolated.

The Discovery

Arsenic was first isolated around 1250 by Albertus Magnus, who heated arsenic trisulfide with soap. Its name derives from the Greek "arsenikon," meaning "yellow orpiment."

Periodic Table Impact

Arsenic’s position in group 15 highlights its metalloid nature, transitional between nitrogen and antimony.

Applications

Arsenic’s properties have led to both beneficial and harmful uses:

1. Pesticides

Historically used as an insecticide and herbicide, now largely phased out.

2. Semiconductors

Gallium arsenide (GaAs) is critical in high-speed electronics and solar cells.

3. Wood Preservation

Chromated copper arsenate (CCA) treated lumber, though its use is declining.

4. Medicine

Arsenic trioxide treats certain leukemias in modern pharmacology.

5. Pigments

Once used in paints and dyes, now restricted due to toxicity.

6. Alloys

Strengthens lead in batteries and ammunition.

Arsenic in the Universe

Arsenic is formed in stars via nucleosynthesis and is present in trace amounts in cosmic dust and meteorites, mirroring its terrestrial occurrence.

Modern Production

Arsenic is obtained as a byproduct of copper, lead, and gold mining, extracted from arsenopyrite via roasting and chemical processing. China, Morocco, and Russia are key producers.

Safety

Arsenic is highly toxic, causing acute poisoning or chronic diseases like cancer with prolonged exposure. It requires strict regulation and handling precautions.

Conclusion

Arsenic, a metalloid with a dual legacy of poison and promise, continues to shape science and industry despite its hazardous nature.
