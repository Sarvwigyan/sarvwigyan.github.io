Tellurium

Introduction

Tellurium is a chemical element with the symbol Te and atomic number 52. It is a brittle, silvery-white metalloid that is primarily used in alloys and as a semiconductor. Tellurium is relatively rare in the Earth's crust and is often found in combination with other elements.

The name "tellurium" is derived from the Latin word "tellus," meaning "earth."

Isotopes

Tellurium has several isotopes, with 130Te being the most stable and abundant, making up about 34.08% of natural tellurium. Other stable isotopes include 128Te, 126Te, and 125Te. Tellurium also has several radioactive isotopes, the most notable being 127Te, which has a half-life of about 4.2 million years.

Properties

Tellurium is a brittle metalloid with a metallic luster. It is a poor conductor of electricity at room temperature but becomes a good conductor when heated. Tellurium is also resistant to oxidation and can form various compounds with other elements.

Physical Properties

Tellurium has a density of 6.24 g/cm³, a melting point of 449.51°C (841.12°F), and a boiling point of 988°C (1810°F). Its crystalline structure is hexagonal.

Chemical Properties

Tellurium reacts with oxygen, forming tellurium dioxide (TeO₂), and can also react with halogens and acids. It exhibits oxidation states of -2, +2, +4, and +6, with the +4 state being the most stable in aqueous solutions.

History

Tellurium was discovered in 1782 by the Hungarian chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth, who isolated it from a gold ore. The element was named after the Earth, reflecting its terrestrial origin.

Applications

Tellurium's unique properties make it valuable in several applications:

1. Alloys

Tellurium is used to improve the machinability of copper and steel alloys.

2. Semiconductors

Tellurium is used in the production of cadmium telluride (CdTe) solar cells and other semiconductor devices.

3. Thermoelectric Devices

Tellurium is used in thermoelectric materials for converting heat into electricity.

4. Chemical Reactions

Tellurium compounds are used as catalysts in various chemical reactions.

Tellurium in the Universe

Tellurium is formed in stars through nucleosynthesis processes. Its cosmic abundance is relatively low, estimated at about 0.0000001% of the universe's mass.

Modern Production

Tellurium is primarily extracted from the refining of copper and lead ores. Major producers of tellurium include China, Japan, and the United States.

Safety

Tellurium is generally considered safe for use, but exposure to tellurium compounds can cause respiratory irritation and other health issues. Safety protocols should be followed when handling tellurium and its compounds.

Conclusion

Tellurium's unique properties and applications in various industries highlight its importance in modern technology. Its role in semiconductors and alloys makes it a valuable element in both practical and technological contexts.
