Selenium

Introduction

Selenium is a chemical element with the symbol Se and atomic number 34. It is a non-metal that exists in several allotropes, including a gray metallic form and a red amorphous form. Selenium is known for its photoconductive properties and is used in various applications, including electronics, glassmaking, and as a dietary supplement.

The element was discovered in 1817 by the Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius and his assistant Johan Gottlieb Gahn while they were studying the mineral pyrite. The name "selenium" is derived from the Greek word "selene," meaning "moon," in reference to its connection with tellurium, which is named after the Earth.

Isotopes

Selenium has several isotopes, with 78Se, 80Se, and 82Se being the most stable and abundant. The average atomic mass of selenium is approximately 78.971 u. Selenium-79 (79Se) is a radioactive isotope with a half-life of about 16 million years and is of interest in nuclear waste management.

Properties

Selenium is a non-metal that exhibits properties of both metals and non-metals. It is a brittle, grayish-black solid in its most stable form and can also exist as a red powder.

Physical Properties

Selenium has a density of 4.81 g/cm³, a melting point of 221°C (430°F), and a boiling point of 685°C (1265°F). Its crystalline structure can vary depending on the allotrope.

Chemical Properties

Selenium reacts with metals to form selenides and can also react with halogens and sulfur. It exhibits oxidation states of -2, +2, +4, and +6, with the -2 state being the most stable in compounds.

History

Selenium was discovered in 1817 by Jöns Jacob Berzelius and Johan Gottlieb Gahn while they were studying the mineral pyrite. The element was isolated and its unique properties were studied extensively. Selenium has since been used in various applications, particularly in electronics and glassmaking.

Applications

Selenium's unique properties make it valuable in several applications:

1. Electronics

Selenium is used in photocells, light meters, and solar cells due to its photoconductive properties.

2. Glassmaking

Selenium is used to decolorize glass and to produce colored glass, particularly in the production of red glass.

3. Dietary Supplement

Selenium is an essential trace element in the human diet and is important for various biological functions, including antioxidant activity.

4. Research

Selenium is used in various scientific research applications, particularly in studies related to its chemical properties and biological effects.

Selenium in the Universe

Selenium is formed in stars through nucleosynthesis processes. It is relatively rare in the universe, with an estimated cosmic abundance of about 0.0007% relative to hydrogen.

Modern Production

Selenium is primarily extracted from copper refining and can also be found in certain minerals, such as selenite and selenide ores. Major producers of selenium include China, Japan, and the United States.

Safety

Selenium is generally considered safe in small amounts, but excessive exposure can lead to toxicity. It is important to follow safety protocols when handling selenium and its compounds.

Conclusion

Selenium's unique properties and applications in various industries highlight its importance in modern technology. As research continues, its role in advanced materials and biological systems is likely to expand.
