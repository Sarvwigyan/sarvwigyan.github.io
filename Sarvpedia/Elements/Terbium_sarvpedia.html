Terbium

Introduction

Terbium is a chemical element with the symbol Tb and atomic number 65. It is a silvery-gray metal that is part of the lanthanide series of the periodic table. Terbium is known for its use in various applications, including phosphors, magnets, and as a dopant in solid-state devices.

The element was discovered in 1843 by Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius and his assistant, who isolated it from the mineral gadolinite. The name "terbium" is derived from the village of Ytterby in Sweden, where several rare earth elements were first discovered.

Isotopes

Terbium has several isotopes, with 159Tb being the most stable and abundant, accounting for nearly 100% of natural terbium. The average atomic mass of terbium is approximately 158.92535 u. Terbium-160 (160Tb) is a radioactive isotope with a half-life of about 72 hours.

Properties

Terbium is a soft, malleable metal that is relatively stable in air. It can oxidize when exposed to moisture and is known for its magnetic properties.

Physical Properties

Terbium has a density of 8.229 g/cm³, a melting point of 1356°C (2473°F), and a boiling point of 3123°C (5653°F). Its crystalline structure is hexagonal close-packed.

Chemical Properties

Terbium reacts with oxygen to form terbium oxide (Tb₂O₃) and can also react with acids to form terbium salts. It exhibits oxidation states of +3 and +4, with the +3 state being the most stable in aqueous solutions.

2. Magnets

3. Solid-State Devices

Terbium is used as a dopant in solid-state devices, improving their performance and efficiency.

4. Research
