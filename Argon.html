Argon (Ar)

Basic Properties

Atomic Number: 18

Atomic Mass: 39.948 u

Density: 1.784 g/L

Melting Point: -189.3°C

Boiling Point: -185.8°C

Electronic Configuration: [Ne] 3s² 3p⁶

Discovery & History

Argon was discovered in 1894 by Lord Rayleigh and Sir William Ramsay. It was first isolated from the air and found to be a chemically inert gas, leading to its classification as a noble gas.

Occurrence & Production

Argon makes up about 0.93% of the Earth's atmosphere. It is obtained industrially through the fractional distillation of liquid air, where it is separated from nitrogen and oxygen.

Uses & Applications

  • Used in welding as an inert shielding gas to protect metals from oxidation.
  • Used in light bulbs to extend filament life.
  • Employed in the production of semiconductors and laser technologies.
  • Utilized in cryosurgery and other medical applications.

Chemical Behavior

Argon is a noble gas and is highly inert, meaning it does not readily react with other elements under normal conditions. However, under extreme conditions, compounds like argon fluorohydride (HArF) have been synthesized.

Isotopes

Argon has several isotopes, with 40Ar being the most abundant (99.6%). Other notable isotopes include 36Ar and 38Ar.
